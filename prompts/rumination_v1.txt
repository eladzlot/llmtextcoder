You are an expert evaluator of written text.

Your task is to assess **rumination** in the following passage.

**Definition**

Rumination is **repetitive negative thinking that remains cognitively stuck, repeatedly revisiting distress without meaningful progression in understanding, emotional processing, or action.**

Assess only what is observable in the text. Do **not** infer diagnoses, personality, mental illness, or facts not explicitly supported by the writing.

---

## Rating Dimensions

Rate each dimension from **1 (low)** to **5 (high)**. Higher scores always indicate more ruminative qualities.

---

### 1. repetition

Does the text repeatedly return to the same thought, emotion, image, fear, or interpretation without adding meaningful new information?

**Score 1** — Little or no repetition. The narrative develops naturally; each sentence adds something new.

> "I was anxious when I arrived. Then I described what happened, how I reacted, and what I learned."

**Score 3** — The text returns to similar themes more than once, but new details or perspectives appear between revisits.

> "I kept thinking about the argument. Later I wondered if I'd misread the situation. The memory came back again when I was trying to sleep."

**Score 5** — The text repeatedly circles the same concern. Similar thoughts are restated with minimal development.

> "It was my fault. I keep thinking it was my fault. Everything comes back to being my fault."

---

### 2. negative fixation

How persistently is attention locked onto negative material — threat, loss, guilt, shame, failure, helplessness, or catastrophe?

Do **not** score high simply because the text describes a traumatic or upsetting event. Score the **degree of persistent negative focus**, not the severity of the event.

**Score 1** — Negative material is minimal or balanced with neutral, concrete, or adaptive content.

> "I felt scared, but I focused on describing exactly what happened."

**Score 3** — Negative material is substantial and recurrent, but moments of neutral description, forward focus, or adaptive reflection are also present.

> "I kept returning to how unfair it felt. Parts of the account were hard to write. But I also tried to remember what I'd done well."

**Score 5** — Nearly the entire passage is dominated by negative interpretation or catastrophic thinking. Neutral or adaptive content is absent or fleeting.

> "Everything is ruined. I'll never recover. Nothing will ever improve."

---

### 3. stagnation

Does the writing move toward understanding, emotional processing, acceptance, or action — or does it remain stuck?

High scores indicate **lack of progress**. This dimension focuses on narrative arc across the passage, not on whether a resolution is reached.

**Score 1** — Clear movement. The writer reaches a new understanding, reframes the event, or identifies next steps.

> "At first I blamed myself, but while writing I realized I was mostly frightened."

**Score 3** — Limited movement. There are small shifts in framing or emotional tone, but no clear arc toward insight or resolution.

> "I'm not sure what to make of it. Maybe I overreacted. But it still bothers me in a way I can't quite name."

**Score 5** — No meaningful movement. The text revisits the same problem without advancing. The end is as stuck as the beginning.

> "Why did this happen? I don't know. I keep wondering why. I'll never understand."

---

### 4. passivity

Does the writer describe themselves as passive, trapped, overwhelmed, or unable to disengage from intrusive thoughts?

High scores reflect **loss of agency over one's own mental processes** — being driven by thoughts rather than directing them. This is distinct from negative emotional content: a writer can describe great distress while remaining an active agent, and can feel passive without describing intense emotion.

**Score 1** — Active stance. The writer observes, reflects, chooses, or deliberately directs attention.

> "I noticed the thought returning and redirected my attention to describing the memory."

**Score 3** — Mixed. The writer sometimes feels overwhelmed or caught, but also shows deliberate reflection or moments of redirection.

> "The thoughts kept pulling me back. I tried to focus on what had actually happened, though I didn't always succeed."

**Score 5** — Strong sense of being controlled by thoughts or emotions. The writer cannot disengage; thoughts feel autonomous and uncontrollable.

> "The thoughts wouldn't stop. I couldn't escape them. They completely controlled me."

---

### 5. abstractness

Does the writing rely on abstract judgments, generalized conclusions, or evaluative thinking rather than concrete, specific, sensory description?

**Important:** Abstract thinking **alone is not rumination** — score only the mode of processing, not its emotional tone. A text can be highly abstract without being negative (e.g., philosophical reflection), and highly negative without being abstract (e.g., a vivid, concrete trauma account). The key question is whether the writer is *generalizing and evaluating* versus *observing and describing*.

**Score 1** — Mostly concrete and specific. The text is grounded in sensory detail, actions, or particular events.

> "I heard the door slam. My hands were shaking. I walked into the room."

**Score 3** — A mix of concrete and abstract. Specific details appear alongside evaluative conclusions or generalizations.

> "It happened so fast — I remember the sound and the smell of the room. Looking back, I think I just shut down when things got overwhelming."

**Score 5** — Mostly abstract, evaluative, and generalized. Self-referential conclusions or global judgments dominate; concrete detail is sparse.

> "I never handle pressure well. I always freeze when it matters. That's just the kind of person I am."

---

## Important Guidelines

* Do **not** equate emotional intensity with rumination.
* Do **not** equate trauma exposure with rumination.
* Concrete emotional engagement often reflects healthy processing, not rumination.
* High rumination requires evidence of repetitive negative stuckness.
* Use the entire passage when assigning scores.
* Each rationale should be concise (10–30 words) and refer only to observable features of the text.
